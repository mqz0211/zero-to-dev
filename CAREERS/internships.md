# Internships

## When to start applying
Earlier than feels comfortable. Many companies open internship
applications 6–9 months before the start date, especially larger ones.
Track deadlines instead of waiting until you feel "ready" — you'll
almost never feel fully ready, and that's normal.

## Where to look
- **University career center** — often has exclusive employer relationships
- **[LinkedIn Jobs](https://www.linkedin.com/jobs/)** — filter by "Internship"
- **Company career pages directly** — for companies you're specifically
  interested in, check monthly; some don't post elsewhere
- **Hackathons** — many have recruiters attending specifically to find interns
- **[Handshake](https://joinhandshake.com)** — if your school has access,
  it's often under-used and less competitive than LinkedIn

## What actually gets you an interview
In rough order of impact for a student with no prior experience:
1. **Projects that demonstrate real skill**, not tutorial clones — see
   [`portfolio.md`](portfolio.md)
2. **A resume that's specific**, not generic — "built a REST API with
   JWT authentication serving 3 endpoints" beats "learned backend development"
3. **Referrals** — reaching out to alumni or people in roles you want,
   genuinely, not as a form letter
4. **Open source contributions** — even small ones (a fixed typo in
   docs counts as a start; a merged bug fix counts a lot)

## The application itself
- **Tailor your resume** to the specific role — reorder bullet points
  to match what the posting emphasizes
- **Apply even if you don't meet every listed requirement** — internship
  postings routinely overstate requirements
- **Track your applications** — a simple spreadsheet: company, role,
  date applied, status, follow-up date

## Interview prep
- **Technical screens:** practice on [NeetCode](https://neetcode.io) or
  similar — aim for comfort with easy/medium problems, explained out loud
- **Behavioral questions:** prepare 3–4 stories from your projects using
  a structure like "situation, task, action, result" — practice saying
  them out loud, not just thinking them
- **Questions to ask them:** always have 2–3 ready — it signals genuine interest

## If you don't hear back
Most applications don't get a response — this is normal, not a
reflection of your ability. Follow up once, politely, after 1–2 weeks
if there's no response, then move on. Volume plus quality beats waiting
on any single application.

## If you don't get an internship this cycle
- Personal/open-source projects are a real substitute on a resume —
  frame them the same way you'd frame work experience
- Apply again next cycle — most students don't land their first
  internship on the first attempt, and it doesn't limit later opportunities
